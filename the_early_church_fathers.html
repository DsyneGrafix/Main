Building the Bible: Canon Formation

The biblical canon was not created by a single decree but was formed through a gradual process of community consensus, which the Church Fathers helped to recognize and formalize. This process affirmed which texts were already widely used and accepted as authoritative in churches across the world.

1. Hebrew Scriptures (Old Testament)

The early Church accepted the sacred writings of Israel as their own authoritative scripture from the very beginning.

↓

2. Apostolic Writings (c. 1st-2nd C.)

The Gospels and the letters of apostles like Paul were copied and circulated, gaining authoritative status in worship and teaching. Fathers like Irenaeus championed the four-Gospel canon against Gnostic alternatives.

↓

3. Formal Recognition (c. 4th C.)

In his Festal Letter of 367 AD, Athanasius of Alexandria provided the first list of New Testament books that exactly matches the 27 books used today, reflecting a broad consensus already in place.

↓

4. Conciliar Affirmation (Late 4th C.)

Councils such as the Synod of Hippo (393) and Council of Carthage (397) officially ratified the 27-book canon articulated by Athanasius, solidifying it for the Western Church.
