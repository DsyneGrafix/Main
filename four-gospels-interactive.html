The Four Gospels

Historical Context, Theological Themes, and Literary Structure

Introduction

The New Testament's four canonical gospels – Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John – each present unique accounts of Jesus Christ's life, teachings, death, and resurrection. Composed in the 1st century A.D. for different audiences and contexts, each Gospel offers distinct theological emphases and literary approaches.

The first three (Matthew, Mark, Luke) are called the "Synoptic Gospels" due to their similar narrative structure, while John's Gospel stands apart with its unique theological depth and symbolic language.

The Gospel of Mark

Author John Mark (Peter's companion)
Date ~66-70 AD
Audience Roman Christians
Key Theme Suffering Messiah

Historical Context

▼

The Gospel of Mark is widely considered the earliest gospel, likely written around the time of the Jewish War against Rome (circa A.D. 66–70). The text quotes Jesus predicting the Temple's destruction (Mark 13:2), suggesting composition near this pivotal event.

Key Point: Early church tradition links this gospel to John Mark, companion of Peter, possibly written in Rome capturing Peter's eyewitness accounts. Mark's community likely faced persecution and confusion, needing affirmation of Jesus's identity amid turmoil.

Theological Themes

▼

Mark emphasizes Jesus as the Messiah and Son of God who must suffer and die as part of God's redemptive plan. A dominant motif is the "Messianic Secret" – Jesus often silences demons and followers from proclaiming his identity.

The Theology of the Cross: Only at the crucifixion does a Roman centurion exclaim "Truly this man was the Son of God!" (Mark 15:39), revealing that Jesus's divine sonship is understood through his suffering and death.

Mark's gospel offers hope by showing that Christ's humiliation was the path to victory, encouraging believers that faith may entail hardship but will ultimately be vindicated in resurrection.

Literary Structure

▼

Mark is the shortest gospel with a fast-paced, dramatic style, frequently using "immediately." The three-act structure includes:

  • Act 1 (1:14–8:26): Jesus's ministry in Galilee
  • Act 2 (8:27–10:52): Journey toward Jerusalem with passion predictions
  • Act 3 (11:1–16:8): Final week in Jerusalem, crucifixion, and resurrection

Mark's vivid storytelling includes unique details like Jesus's emotions, giving it an eyewitness quality consistent with Peter's reminiscences.

The Gospel of Matthew

Author Matthew (Tax Collector)
Date ~80-90 AD
Audience Jewish Christians
Key Theme Jesus as New Moses

Historical Context

▼

Matthew was composed around A.D. 80–90, likely in Syrian Antioch or another Eastern Mediterranean center with a substantial Jewish-Christian population. The author assumes readers understand Jewish customs without explanation.

Key Point: Written for a Jewish-Christian community recently expelled from or at odds with the synagogue, navigating between Jewish tradition and the emerging Gentile mission. Matthew demonstrates Jesus as the fulfillment of God's promises to Israel.

Theological Themes

▼

Matthew is the most "Jewish" gospel, presenting Jesus as the Messiah predicted in Hebrew Scriptures. Key themes include:

  • Fulfillment Quotations: "This took place to fulfill what was spoken by the prophet..." (appears ~12 times)
  • Jesus as New Moses: Five major teaching discourses parallel the five books of Torah
  • Higher Righteousness: "You have heard...but I say to you..." intensifying the Law's requirements
  • Royal Messiah: "Son of David, son of Abraham" establishing Jesus's kingly lineage
The Church: Only Gospel to explicitly mention "church" (ekklesia) - "On this rock I will build my church" (16:18)

Literary Structure

▼

Matthew alternates between narrative and discourse sections, with five major teaching blocks:

  1. Sermon on the Mount (Ch. 5-7)
  2. Missionary Instructions (Ch. 10)
  3. Parable Discourse (Ch. 13)
  4. Community Discipline (Ch. 18)
  5. Olivet Discourse (Ch. 24-25)

Each discourse ends with "When Jesus had finished these sayings..." Matthew organizes material in groups of threes and sevens for memorization.

The Gospel of Luke

Author Luke the Physician
Date ~80-90 AD
Audience Gentile Christians
Key Theme Universal Salvation

Historical Context

▼

Luke is the first volume of Luke-Acts, written by Paul's companion "the beloved physician." Composed in educated Greek with a formal historical prologue, it was likely written in a Gentile milieu outside Palestine.

Key Point: Addressed to Theophilus but intended for Gentile Christians throughout the Mediterranean. Luke's genealogy traces Jesus back to Adam (not just Abraham), emphasizing Jesus as Savior for all humanity.

Theological Themes

▼

Luke's distinctive themes include:

  • Social Justice: Concern for the poor, women, sinners, Samaritans, and outcasts
  • Mercy & Forgiveness: Parables of Good Samaritan, Prodigal Son, forgiveness on the cross
  • Holy Spirit: Active from birth narratives through Jesus's ministry
  • Prayer: Jesus praying at key moments, parables on prayer
  • Joy: Frequent rejoicing when the lost are found
Mary's Magnificat: God "has exalted the lowly and filled the hungry...sent the rich away empty" - theme of reversal permeating the Gospel.

Literary Structure

▼

Luke presents the most comprehensive chronological narrative:

  • Prologue & Infancy (1:1–2:52): Births of John and Jesus
  • Galilean Ministry (4:14–9:50): Teaching and miracles
  • Journey to Jerusalem (9:51–19:27): Unique "Travel Narrative" with many parables
  • Jerusalem & Resurrection (19:28–24:53): Final week and appearances

Luke begins and ends in Jerusalem, tying to Acts where the gospel spreads from Jerusalem to Rome.

🦅

The Gospel of John

Author Beloved Disciple (John)
Date ~90-100 AD
Audience Mixed Jewish-Gentile
Key Theme Jesus as Divine Word

Historical Context

▼

John is the latest Gospel (A.D. 90-100), likely written in Ephesus. Attributed to "the disciple whom Jesus loved," it emerged from the Johannine community during conflicts with synagogues and early heresies.

Key Evidence: Papyrus P52 (c. 125 AD) is the oldest NT manuscript fragment, containing John 18, proving the Gospel's early circulation throughout the Mediterranean.

Theological Themes

▼

John presents the highest Christology with unique features:

  • Divine Identity: "In the beginning was the Word...the Word was God" (1:1)
  • Seven "I Am" Statements: Bread of Life, Light of the World, Good Shepherd, etc.
  • Seven Signs: Miracles revealing Jesus's glory (water to wine → raising Lazarus)
  • Dualistic Imagery: Light/darkness, truth/falsehood, life/death
  • Love & Unity: New commandment, Farewell Discourse (Ch. 13-17)
Purpose Statement: "These are written that you may believe Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and by believing have life in his name" (20:31)

Literary Structure

▼

John's structure differs from the Synoptics:

  1. Prologue (1:1-18): Cosmic introduction of the Word
  2. Book of Signs (1:19-12:50): Seven miracles and discourses
  3. Book of Glory (13:1-20:31): Farewell Discourse, Passion, Resurrection
  4. Epilogue (Ch. 21): Beach appearance and Peter's restoration

John uses misunderstandings as teaching opportunities, simple vocabulary with deep symbolism, and emphasizes Jewish festivals connecting to Jesus's identity.

The Synoptic Problem

Matthew, Mark, and Luke share similar content and structure (hence "Synoptic" = seeing together). Most scholars believe Mark was written first, with Matthew and Luke using Mark plus a shared sayings source ("Q") plus their own unique materials.

Why Four Gospels?

Each Gospel provides a complementary perspective, like four portraits of the same person. Together they offer a rich, multifaceted understanding of Jesus's identity and mission - historical facts preserved alongside theological meaning for different communities.
