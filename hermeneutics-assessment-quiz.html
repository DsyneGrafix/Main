Biblical Hermeneutics Assessment

Test Your Understanding of Scripture Interpretation

Instructions: Answer all questions to the best of your ability. This assessment is designed to reinforce learning, not to trick you. Take your time and apply the principles you've learned. (Total: 20 points)
Question 1 (2 points)
Define "biblical hermeneutics" in one clear sentence.
Question 2 (2 points)
Which principle of interpretation helps us understand that Psalm 23 uses metaphorical language rather than literal shepherding instructions?
Question 3 (2 points)
True or False: A proverb in the Bible should always be taken as an absolute promise that applies to every situation.
Question 4 (2 points)
List the five core principles of biblical interpretation (one point for getting at least 3 correct).
Question 5 (2 points)
When you encounter a difficult or unclear passage, which principle suggests using clearer passages to help understand it?
Question 6 (2 points)
Explain why Philippians 4:13 ("I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me") is NOT primarily about achieving personal goals. Use the principle of context in your answer.
Question 7 (2 points)
Which method of Bible study follows this pattern: Observe → Interpret → Apply?
Question 8 (2 points)
True or False: Understanding the original language (Hebrew/Greek) requires years of study and is essential before anyone can properly interpret the Bible.
Question 9 (2 points)
In one sentence, reconcile this apparent contradiction: Paul says we're saved "by faith apart from works" (Romans 3:28) while James says "a person is justified by works and not by faith alone" (James 2:24).
Question 10 (2 points)
What is the FIRST thing you should do when you encounter a Bible verse you want to understand better?

ANSWER KEY - Facilitator's Copy

Question 1 (2 points)
Acceptable Answer: Biblical hermeneutics is the art and science of interpreting Scripture to understand its original meaning and contemporary application.
Also accept variations that mention: interpretation, understanding meaning, bridging ancient/modern contexts. Partial credit (1 point) for answers that only mention interpretation without the dual aspect.
Question 2 (2 points)
Answer: B) Genre
Psalms are Hebrew poetry, which uses metaphor and imagery. Recognizing genre prevents literal misinterpretation.
Question 3 (2 points)
Answer: False
Proverbs are wisdom literature offering general principles, not absolute promises. They describe what usually happens, not what always happens.
Question 4 (2 points)
The Five Core Principles: 1. Context (Context is King) 2. Genre (Genre Shapes Meaning) 3. Original Language 4. Authorial Intent 5. Scripture Interprets Scripture
Full credit for all 5 correct (any order). 1 point for 3-4 correct. Accept reasonable variations in wording.
Question 5 (2 points)
Answer: C) Scripture Interprets Scripture
This principle (also called the analogy of faith) uses clear passages to illuminate unclear ones.
Question 6 (2 points)
Model Answer: In context (Phil 4:10-13), Paul writes from prison about learning contentment in all circumstances—whether in plenty or want. The "all things" refers to enduring any situation through Christ's strength, not achieving personal goals.
Full credit for mentioning: prison context, contentment theme, or endurance vs achievement. Partial credit for incomplete but correct understanding.
Question 7 (2 points)
Answer: B) Inductive Bible Study
The inductive method moves from observation (what it says) to interpretation (what it means) to application (how it applies).
Question 8 (2 points)
Answer: False
While helpful, original languages can be accessed through tools like lexicons and interlinears. Good translations and study tools make interpretation accessible to all.
Question 9 (2 points)
Model Answer: Paul addresses how we're saved (by faith, not by earning it through works), while James addresses how genuine saving faith behaves (it produces good works as evidence).
Accept any answer that distinguishes between: - Justification by faith vs. evidence of faith - Root of salvation vs. fruit of salvation - Faith alone saves, but saving faith is never alone
Question 10 (2 points)
Answer: B) Read the surrounding context
Context is the starting point for interpretation. Never read a Bible verse—always read the paragraph or chapter.
