1) How to Use This Guide

This resource is built for rapid orientation and deep study. Each journey section includes a text-map (a simple arrow-linked route), the Acts passages, letter connections, and a why it matters note for discipleship and mission.

2) The Big Picture: Paul’s Life in Twelve Movements

  1. Tarsus & Jerusalem formation (Acts 21:39; 22:3): Hebrew of Hebrews, educated under Gamaliel.
  2. Persecutor → convert (Acts 9; 22; 26): Damascus Road encounter with the risen Jesus.
  3. Early years (Arabia, Damascus, Tarsus): obscurity, formation, local ministry.
  4. Antioch team (Acts 11): Barnabas recruits Saul; a multiethnic church becomes a mission base.
  5. Journey One (Acts 13–14): Cyprus & South Galatia; Gentile doors swing open.
  6. Jerusalem Council (Acts 15): gospel of grace affirmed for Gentiles.
  7. Journey Two (Acts 15–18): Macedonia & Achaia; Philippi → Thessalonica → Berea → Athens → Corinth.
  8. Journey Three (Acts 18–21): Ephesus hub; collection for Jerusalem; strengthening churches.
  9. Arrest & trials (Acts 21–26): Jerusalem tumult; Caesarea hearings before Felix, Festus, Agrippa.
  10. Voyage to Rome (Acts 27–28): storm, shipwreck on Malta, gospel in Rome.
  11. Years after Acts? (inference from Pastorals): further travels, co-workers, leadership handoffs.
  12. Final witness: tradition points to martyrdom in Rome; “fought the good fight” (2 Tim 4:7–8).

3) Timeline & Chronology (Approximate)

Scholars offer multiple chronologies. This one uses a mid-30s conversion and anchors dates around Gallio’s proconsulship in Corinth (~AD 51–52; Acts 18:12), which is widely used as a fixed point.

~AD 5–10 — Birth in Tarsus (Acts 22:3; Phil 3:5).

~AD 20–34 — Jerusalem training; rising Pharisee; persecutes the church.

~AD 34–36 — Damascus Road; Arabia/Damascus (Gal 1:17).

~AD 36–45 — Tarsus years; Barnabas later brings Saul to Antioch (Acts 11:25–26).

~AD 46–47 — Journey 1 (Acts 13–14); likely writes Galatians soon after (debated).

AD 49 — Jerusalem Council (Acts 15).

~AD 49–52 — Journey 2 (Acts 15:36–18:22); in Corinth ~18 months; writes 1–2 Thessalonians.

~AD 52–57 — Journey 3 (Acts 18:23–21:17); Ephesian hub ~3 years; writes 1–2 Corinthians, Romans.

~AD 57–59 — Arrest; Caesarea imprisonment (Acts 23–26).

~AD 59–60 — Voyage to Rome; shipwreck on Malta (Acts 27–28).

~AD 60–62 — House arrest in Rome; writes Philippians, Philemon, Colossians, Ephesians.

~AD 64–67 — Later travels? Pastoral Epistles context; tradition of martyrdom in Rome.

Note: You will find scholarly alternatives that shift these a few years earlier or later. The theological weight of Paul’s message does not depend on a precise year-by-year alignment.

5) Origins: Tarsus, Jerusalem, and the Damascus Encounter

Tarsus gave Paul bilingual, bicultural fluency—Greek urban life and Jewish home training (Acts 21:39; 22:3). In Jerusalem, he studied under Gamaliel, a respected Pharisee (Acts 22:3). Zeal led him to persecute followers of “the Way” (Acts 8:3; 9:1–2), until the risen Jesus confronted him on the Damascus road (Acts 9; retold in Acts 22; 26).

  • Damascus & Arabia (Gal 1:17): a period of recalibration; he returns to Damascus.
  • First Jerusalem visit (Gal 1:18; Acts 9:26–30): meets Peter and James; faces danger; sent to Tarsus.
  • Tarsus/Cilicia years: quiet ministry; preparation in obscurity.

Text-Map: Early Movements

Jerusalem → (on the road) → Damascus → Arabia → Damascus → Jerusalem → Caesarea → Tarsus

Why it matters: Paul’s story is not an instant platform. He spends years learning to follow Jesus before leading others far afield. Calling grows in hiddenness.

6) The Antioch Years: Team Formation & First Commission

Antioch becomes the Gentile mission hub (Acts 11:19–30; 13:1–3). Barnabas recruits Saul from Tarsus (Acts 11:25–26). A multiethnic leadership team fasts and prays; the Spirit sets apart Barnabas and Saul (Acts 13:1–3).

Text-Map: Antioch Base

Tarsus → Antioch (team formed, teaching, relief to Jerusalem) → Antioch (commissioned)

Why it matters: Antioch models a sending church—worship, fasting, discernment, generosity, and a team culture that multiplies leaders.

7) Journey One (Acts 13–14): Cyprus & South Galatia

Scripture: Acts 13:4–14:28. Companions: Barnabas, John Mark (who later returns to Jerusalem), and later Paul/Barnabas appoint local elders.

Text-Map

Antioch (Syria) → Seleucia → Salamis (Cyprus) → Paphos → Perga (Pamphylia) → Pisidian Antioch → Iconium → Lystra → Derbe → back through Lystra → Iconium → Pisidian Antioch → Perga → Attalia → Antioch (Syria)

  • Paphos: confrontation with Elymas; proconsul believes (Acts 13:6–12).
  • Pisidian Antioch: synagogue address; Gentiles rejoice; opposition rises.
  • Lystra: healing of a lame man; mistaken as gods; stoning; resilience (Acts 14).

Letters connection: Galatians may date shortly after (South Galatian hypothesis), addressing Gentile inclusion and justification by faith (Gal 2–3).

Why it matters: The gospel is for the nations. Suffering and joy mingle as churches are planted and elders appointed in each city (Acts 14:23).

8) Jerusalem Council (Acts 15): The Gospel Clarified

Do Gentile believers need to become Jews to follow Jesus? The Council affirms salvation by grace through faith in Christ, without imposing the full Mosaic law on Gentiles (Acts 15:1–21). A letter brings unity and practical guidance (Acts 15:22–35).

Takeaway: The gospel’s center is Jesus—His grace, not boundary markers. Unity is protected by listening, Scripture, testimony, and Spirit-led consensus.

9) Journey Two (Acts 15–18): Macedonia & Achaia

Scripture: Acts 15:36–18:22. Companions: Silas, Timothy; later Aquila & Priscilla join the story; Luke appears (“we” sections).

Text-Map

Antioch (Syria) → Syria–Cilicia (strengthen churches) → Derbe → Lystra (Timothy joins) → Iconium → Troas → Philippi [Acts 16] → Thessalonica → Berea → Athens → Corinth [Acts 18] → Cenchreae → Ephesus (brief) → Caesarea → Jerusalem → Antioch

  • Troas: “Macedonian vision” redirects plans (Acts 16:9–10).
  • Philippi: Lydia’s household; jailer’s conversion; gospel takes root.
  • Athens: Areopagus address; contextual mission in a plural setting (Acts 17).
  • Corinth: 18 months; Gallio’s ruling provides legal cover (Acts 18:12–17).

Letters connection: 1–2 Thessalonians likely from Corinth; hope, holiness, and the Lord’s coming amid pressure.

Why it matters: The Spirit guides mission strategy (closed doors, new doors). Urban centers become hubs for regional witness.

10) Journey Three (Acts 18–21): Ephesus and the Collection

Scripture: Acts 18:23–21:17. Companions: a broader network—Timothy, Titus, Trophimus, and others. Ephesus becomes a multi-year base; the “collection” for Jerusalem expresses unity.

Text-Map

Antioch (Syria) → Galatia–Phrygia (strengthen) → Ephesus (long stay) → Macedonia → Greece/Achaia (Corinth) → back through Macedonia → Troas → Miletus (Ephesian elders) → Cos → Rhodes → Patara → Tyre → Ptolemais → Caesarea → Jerusalem

  • Ephesus: disciples receive the Spirit; public impact; dispute with Artemis trade (Acts 19).
  • Troas: Eutychus incident; encouragement and teaching (Acts 20:7–12).
  • Miletus: tearful farewell—shepherd leadership charge (Acts 20:17–38).

Letters connection: During/around this span Paul writes 1–2 Corinthians and Romans. We see pastoral pain, reconciliation, generosity, and a sweeping articulation of the gospel.

Why it matters: Healthy churches need shepherds, not celebrities; generosity unites diverse believers; doctrine fuels endurance and love.

11) Arrest in Jerusalem → Caesarea (Acts 21–26)

Despite warnings, Paul goes to Jerusalem with a conscience to serve and unify (Acts 20–21). A temple misunderstanding sparks a riot; Roman custody follows. He testifies to mobs, councils, and governors (Acts 22–26), appealing to Caesar.

Text-Map

Jerusalem (arrest) → Antonia Fortress → Sanhedrin → transfer to Caesarea (Governor Felix) → hearings with Festus & Agrippa

Why it matters: Paul’s chains become a platform; God’s promise to “carry my name” to rulers comes true (Acts 9:15). Courage is not noise but steady witness.

12) To Rome (Acts 27–28): Storm, Shipwreck, Witness

Under guard, Paul sails for Italy. A northeaster drives the ship to shipwreck on Malta. After wintering there, the party reaches Rome, where Paul preaches “with all boldness and without hindrance” (Acts 28:31).

Text-Map

Caesarea → Sidon → lee of Cyprus → Myra (Lycia) → Cnidus → under Crete to Fair Havens → storm → Malta (winter) → Syracuse → Rhegium → Puteoli → Forum of Appius & Three Taverns → Rome

Letters connection: In Rome (house arrest), Paul writes “prison epistles” (Philippians, Philemon, Colossians, Ephesians)—joy, unity, spiritual warfare, and the cosmic Christ.

Why it matters: Mission is resilient. Storms, delays, and legal processes cannot chain the word of God.

13) After Acts: Possible Spanish Hopes & Pastoral Years

Acts ends with an open door in Rome. Later letters imply further travels (e.g., Titus 1:5; 2 Tim 4:13, 20). Paul hoped to visit Spain (Rom 15:24, 28). The Pastorals depict leadership development and church order in the post-Acts period. Tradition holds Paul was martyred in Rome.

Hold loosely: Details after Acts rely on inference and early church tradition. The thrust remains: preach Christ, form leaders, finish well.

14) Themes Along the Road: Theology in Motion

14.1 Grace & Justification

Faith in Christ unites Jew and Gentile; the law cannot justify (Galatians; Romans 3–5). Freedom expresses itself in love.

14.2 The Spirit & New Life

The Spirit seals, empowers, and bears fruit (Gal 5; Rom 8). Churches are Spirit-formed communities.

14.3 The Church as One Body

Gifts serve unity and mission (1 Cor 12–14; Eph 4). Diverse cultures, one gospel.

14.4 Holiness in a Pagan World

Sexual integrity, honesty, generosity, and work ethic become witness (1 Thess 4; 1 Cor 6; Eph 4–6).

14.5 Suffering & Joy

Hardship is normal, not anomalous; joy is resilient (2 Cor; Phil). Weakness becomes a stage for grace.

14.6 Hope & the Lord’s Coming

Encourage one another with the hope of resurrection and renewal (1–2 Thess; 1 Cor 15).

15) City Notes (Quick Reference)

Antioch (Syria)

  • Sending hub; first called “Christians” (Acts 11:26; 13:1–3).
  • Model of multiethnic leadership and generosity.

Philippi

  • Roman colony; Lydia and the jailer’s households (Acts 16).
  • Warm partnership—see Philippians.

Thessalonica

  • Opposition and endurance (Acts 17:1–9).
  • Letters focus on hope, work, and holiness.

Athens

  • Areopagus address—contextual preaching (Acts 17:16–34).
  • Common ground: creation, conscience, resurrection.

Corinth

  • Commercial crossroads; moral complexity; long stay (Acts 18).
  • Letters teach cross-shaped leadership, unity, love.

Ephesus

  • Regional hub; “all Asia heard the word” (Acts 19:10).
  • Spiritual power encounter; deep discipleship.

Jerusalem

  • Cradle of the church; councils, conflict, and care.
  • Collection symbolizes one family in Christ.

Rome

  • Imperial center; gospel “without hindrance” (Acts 28:31).
  • Letters frame global mission and unity.

18) Quick FAQ

Do we know every stop and date precisely?

No. Acts highlights representative events. Letters add detail. Scholars reconstruct dates around fixed points like Gallio (Acts 18:12). Hold specifics with humility.

Why so much suffering?

Opposition meets the gospel’s advance. Paul teaches that weakness showcases Christ’s power (2 Cor 12). Suffering does not mean failure; often it marks faithfulness.

How can I “see” the routes without images?

Use the text-maps here to build a mental model. If desired, later consult an atlas while keeping these notes for quick recall.

What if I only have 10 minutes a day?

Pair a short Acts passage with one paragraph from a related letter. Ask: What does this teach me about Jesus, the Spirit, the church, and my city?
