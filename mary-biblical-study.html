Mary, the Mother of Jesus

Chosen to Bear the Messiah - A Study of Faith, Humility, and Divine Purpose

Summary of Mary's Story in Scripture

üé®

A 6th-century icon depicting Mary holding the Christ Child. Mary's story unfolds in the New Testament Gospels of Matthew, Luke, John, and briefly in Acts.

She first appears in Nazareth, a town in Galilee, as a young virgin betrothed to a man named Joseph. In this Annunciation scene, the angel Gabriel greets her and announces that she has "found favor with God" and will conceive a son by the power of the Holy Spirit, to be the promised Messiah (Luke 1:30-33).

Understandably startled and confused, Mary asks how this will be since she is a virgin, but when Gabriel explains God's plan, she responds with faithful obedience:

"I am the Lord's servant. May it be done to me according to your word"

- Mary's Fiat (Luke 1:38)

This humble "yes" (often called Mary's fiat) set in motion the incarnation of Jesus Christ. Soon after, Mary visits her relative Elizabeth (who is miraculously pregnant with John the Baptist), and Mary bursts into praise ‚Äî the Magnificat ‚Äî proclaiming "my soul magnifies the Lord... for He has looked on the humble estate of His servant" (Luke 1:46-48).

The Birth of Jesus

The Gospels of Matthew and Luke describe Jesus' birth with Mary playing a central role. Mary travels with Joseph to Bethlehem due to a Roman census, and there, in humble surroundings, she gives birth to Jesus (fulfilling prophecy that the Messiah would be born in David's city).

Luke's Gospel tells of angels announcing the birth to local shepherds, who come to find Mary, Joseph, and the baby lying in a manger (Luke 2:4-7, 16-19). Mary quietly treasures all these events, "pondering them in her heart" (Luke 2:19).

"But Mary treasured up all these things, pondering them in her heart" (Luke 2:19)

Matthew's Gospel adds that sometime later, Magi (wise men from the East) visit and honor the young Jesus. When King Herod seeks to kill the child, Mary and Joseph protect their son by fleeing with him to Egypt until Herod's death. After returning, the Holy Family settles in Nazareth, where Jesus is raised (Matthew 2:19-23, Luke 2:39-40).

Jesus' Ministry and Mary's Presence

The only glimpse of Jesus' childhood beyond the infancy comes when 12-year-old Jesus stays behind in the Jerusalem Temple, and a worried Mary finds him discussing Scripture with the teachers. Though Mary doesn't fully understand Jesus' remark that he "must be about His Father's business," she keeps these words in her heart (Luke 2:49-51).

During Jesus' adult ministry, Mary makes a few significant appearances. At a wedding in Cana, it is Mary who informs Jesus that the hosts have run out of wine, implicitly trusting Him to help. Jesus performs His first miracle there ‚Äî turning water into wine ‚Äî and Mary tells the servants:

Mary's Faith in Action

"Do whatever He tells you" - These words to the servants at Cana (John 2:5) demonstrate Mary's complete trust in Jesus' divine mission and power.

At the Cross

Mary's most poignant appearance is at the Crucifixion. By this time likely widowed, Mary stands near the cross, witnessing her son's suffering. In the Gospel of John, Jesus, in His dying moments, honors His mother by entrusting her to the beloved disciple (traditionally John), saying "Woman, behold your son" and to the disciple, "Behold your mother" (John 19:25-27).

This act not only ensures Mary will be cared for, but also highlights the spiritual family formed at the foot of the cross. After Jesus' death and resurrection, Mary's final appearance in Scripture is in the Book of Acts. In Acts 1:14, after Jesus' ascension, Mary is found in Jerusalem praying in the upper room with the apostles and other believers.

This is a brief but significant note ‚Äî Mary is present at the birth of the Church, persevering in faith and prayer as she always had. Throughout, she is depicted as a model of trust in God's promises, "blessed... among women" (Luke 1:42) for her unique role as the mother of the Messiah.

Character Study: Mary's Faith, Humility, Obedience, and Strength

Mary's character is rich and multifaceted, evolving through the Gospel narratives as she encounters both joy and sorrow.

Faith

Faith is perhaps Mary's most defining trait. From the moment Gabriel greets her, Mary chooses to believe God's word to her. Unlike Zechariah (John's father) who doubted the angel's message and was struck mute, Mary did not ask for a sign but only sought understanding of how God's promise would happen.

This indicates that she trusted it would happen. Gabriel even commends her faith, implicitly contrasting it with disbelief: "Blessed is she who believed" (Luke 1:45, spoken by Elizabeth).

The Magnificat - Mary's Song of Praise

"My soul magnifies the Lord, and my spirit rejoices in God my Savior, for he has looked on the humble estate of his servant. For behold, from now on all generations will call me blessed; for he who is mighty has done great things for me, and holy is his name." (Luke 1:46-49)

Mary's vibrant faith "served to shield her during the challenging events" that would come. She believed that "nothing will be impossible with God" (Luke 1:37) and lived by that conviction.

Humility and Obedience

Coupled with Mary's faith is her profound humility and obedience. She consistently points away from herself and toward God's greatness. When praised by Elizabeth as "blessed among women," Mary deflects all glory to God in her Magnificat: "He who is mighty has done great things for me... He has exalted those of humble estate" (Luke 1:49, 52).

Mary's Humble Response

Mary was "clothed in humility and meekness." There was no sense of entitlement or pride in being chosen; "instead, we find Mary glorifying God while acknowledging her own humble place." She calls herself the Lord's handmaid or servant (Luke 1:38), indicating her willingness to serve God's purpose rather than her own ambition.

Her obedience is immediate and wholehearted ‚Äî "let it be done unto me according to your word" is the response that defines her (Luke 1:38). Throughout Jesus' early years, Mary and Joseph obey God's law and guidance (having Jesus circumcised on the eighth day, presenting Him at the Temple, fleeing to Egypt when warned, etc.), showing Mary's continued submission to God's instructions.

Inner Strength and Courage

Another striking aspect of Mary's character is her inner strength and courage. Though often artistically depicted as gentle and mild, the real Mary showed remarkable fortitude in the face of adversity.

Saying "yes" to God's plan meant Mary risked her reputation and even her safety. In her first step of obedience, she faced the very real prospect of being deemed an unwed mother in a conservative Jewish society ‚Äî a situation that could result in severe punishment or ostracism.

The Cost of Obedience

Indeed, Joseph initially planned to break off the betrothal quietly to avoid scandal (Matthew 1:19) until God intervened. Mary likely endured gossip and suspicion from neighbors, yet she bore it bravely, trusting that God's will was worth any cost. This young woman's courage is evident in how she "faced these difficulties with courage," willingly sharing the angel's news with Joseph and perhaps others, not knowing how they'd react.

Later, Mary had to undertake a difficult journey to Bethlehem in late pregnancy, give birth in a primitive setting, flee as a refugee to Egypt to protect her child ‚Äî all of which required strength and resilience.

Fast forward thirty years, and Mary shows strength of a different sort at the Crucifixion. There she stands as a "silent witness", watching her beloved son tortured and killed. The agony of that moment (foretold by Simeon when he told Mary "a sword will pierce your own soul too," Luke 2:35) is beyond imagining, yet Mary does not collapse in despair or flee.

"A sword will pierce your own soul too" - Simeon's prophecy to Mary (Luke 2:35)

Reflective and Thoughtful Nature

Additionally, Mary demonstrates a reflective, thoughtful personality that contributes to her spiritual depth. On multiple occasions, Luke mentions Mary "treasuring up" events or sayings and pondering them in her heart (Luke 2:19, 2:51).

She does not always react with immediate understanding, but she meditates on God's words and works, seeking to grasp their meaning. This contemplative aspect shows Mary's desire to learn and grow.

Over time, Mary's understanding of Jesus' identity and mission likely deepened. Early on, she knew Jesus was Messiah (the angel had told her), yet like many Jews she may have expected a more immediately victorious Messianic role.

By the time of the cross and resurrection, Mary's role shifts into that of a disciple in the early Church, no longer just Jesus' mother but a believer in Him as Lord. We find Mary praying with the apostles in Acts 1, showing that she had fully embraced Jesus' mission of salvation through the cross and resurrection.

Devotional Reflections and Life Applications

Mary's life story offers rich lessons for believers. Her example speaks to anyone who has felt overwhelmed by God's plans or has had to trust God through uncertainty. Here are key reflections and applications drawn from Mary's journey:

Trusting God's Plan

Mary teaches us the importance of trusting God even when His plan disrupts our own. When the angel announced God's purpose, Mary was initially afraid and had questions ‚Äî "Mary was confused and frightened" by the sudden news. But despite not having all the answers, she chose to trust God's word.

Personal Application

"It's okay to have questions, but trusting God is essential." In our lives, we may face divine detours or callings we don't fully understand. Like Mary, we can believe that God is faithful and yield to His plan, confident that "nothing is impossible with God" (Luke 1:37) and that His purposes are for our ultimate good.

Surrender and Obedience

Along with trust, Mary exemplifies surrendering to God's will. She called herself the Lord's servant and willingly laid aside her own plans for God's higher purpose. Her wholehearted "yes" to God (Luke 1:38) challenges us to respond likewise when God calls.

Surrender might mean accepting a difficult path or yielding control of our future to God. Mary's obedience brought her both hardship and profound blessing ‚Äî she became the mother of the Savior.

The Power of "Yes"

In the same way, saying "yes" to God can lead us into unknown territory, but also into our greatest purpose. We learn from Mary that obedience often requires courage and self-denial, but it allows God to use us in extraordinary ways. As Jesus said, "blessed are those who hear the word of God and keep it" (Luke 11:28). Mary did exactly that, and we're invited to do so as well.

Praise and Thankfulness in All Circumstances

Mary's first instinct after accepting God's plan was to worship. In her Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55), spoken while she was still in a precarious situation, Mary magnified the Lord and rejoiced in God her Savior. She praised God for His mercy, His justice, and the fulfillment of His promises.

This teaches us to praise God even amid uncertainty or trial. Mary could have focused on the potential shame or the challenges ahead, but instead she focused on God's goodness.

Learning from Mary's Praise

"In her song of praise, Mary expressed joy and gratitude to God for His blessings... We should praise God for His faithfulness, even during challenging times." Cultivating a grateful heart like Mary's can bolster our faith when we face difficulties. Singing or praying words of praise shifts our gaze from our problem to the One who is bigger than the problem.

Quiet Strength and Endurance

Finally, Mary models a quiet, resilient strength ‚Äî a steady faith that isn't loud but is unshakable. Throughout many hardships ‚Äî a long journey while pregnant, giving birth in a stable, fleeing persecution, years of normal life waiting for Jesus' mission to unfold, and the supreme grief of the cross ‚Äî Mary bore her trials with remarkable grace and perseverance.

She didn't always speak or act publicly (after the Cana miracle, Mary fades into the background of the Gospel story), but her presence is consistently faithful. At the cross, for example, Mary's silent suffering and continued trust speak volumes.

Standing Firm in Faith

This reminds us that strength is often demonstrated in staying the course and remaining faithful to God over the long haul, rather than in dramatic words or deeds. Mary's "quiet strength" ‚Äî her ability to stand firm in faith through pain, to ponder rather than panic, to pray rather than despair ‚Äî encourages us to cultivate an inner fortitude rooted in faith.

In all these ways, Mary's life is more than just a historical account ‚Äî it's a source of devotional inspiration. She was a young ordinary woman who, by God's grace, lived out an extraordinary faith. As such, Mary invites us to trust deeply, surrender fully, praise continually, and stand firmly in faith.

Historical and Cultural Context

Understanding Mary's world helps illuminate the radical nature of her calling. Mary lived in first-century Judea, a Jewish society under Roman occupation, with customs and expectations very different from today's.

Marriage Customs and Social Expectations

She was likely in her early to mid-teens when her momentous journey began, since it was common for Jewish girls of that time to marry soon after puberty. In fact, marriage customs in that era involved a two-stage process: betrothal followed by formal marriage about a year later.

Understanding Betrothal

Betrothal (Hebrew kiddushin) was much more binding than modern engagement ‚Äî it was a contract between families that made the couple legally husband and wife, though they did not yet live together or consummate the marriage until the wedding ceremony later. As one source explains, betrothal "was absolutely binding... It lasted for one year... [and] could not be terminated except by divorce."

During this period, the bride remained with her parents, and any unfaithfulness was considered adultery under the law. Mary was betrothed to Joseph when the angel visited her (Luke 1:27), which is why Joseph, upon learning Mary was pregnant, considered divorcing her quietly to avoid shaming her (Matthew 1:19).

Young women's age at betrothal was usually around 12-14 years old. Parents were encouraged to arrange a daughter's engagement by around age 12, and to celebrate the marriage about a year later. Historical records (like ancient marriage contracts and inscriptions) confirm girls often married in their mid-teens in that culture.

Social Risks and Courage

Social expectations for young women in Mary's day were centered around family and honor. Jewish women of that era had limited public roles; their primary sphere was the home. They were expected to marry, bear and raise children, and manage the household. A woman's reputation for chastity and faithfulness was paramount ‚Äî any hint of sexual immorality could ruin her life.

The Cost of Mary's Yes

This backdrop highlights how courageous Mary's consent to God's plan was: she risked all social security by accepting a pregnancy that could be seen as scandalous. Under Mosaic Law (Deuteronomy 22:20-21), an unmarried woman found pregnant could even face stoning, though by Mary's time it's likely such extreme punishment was rare. More commonly, she'd face public shame and be unmarriageable ‚Äî a potentially desperate situation.

Knowing this, Mary still declared herself the Lord's servant, trusting God to vindicate her. Indeed, God did protect her ‚Äî through Joseph's obedience to the angel and likely through the support of her family.

Religious and Cultural Context of Messianic Hope

Finally, Mary's story must be understood against the backdrop of the religious and cultural context of Messianic hope in first-century Judaism. Mary grew up in an era when her people ardently awaited the Messiah (Mashiach in Hebrew, meaning "Anointed One").

Jewish scriptures and prophets had long foretold that a deliverer from the line of King David would come. By Mary's time, Judea had been under foreign rule (first the Greeks, then the Romans) for centuries, and the hope for a God-sent king to restore Israel's fortunes was intense.

"Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel" (Isaiah 7:14)

Many Jews expected the Messiah to be a political liberator ‚Äî a king who would free them from Roman oppression and re-establish an independent kingdom of Israel. There were also strands of expectation for a priestly or prophetic Messiah, and figures like the Essenes were reading the Old Testament prophecies to calculate the timing of the Messiah's appearance.

Mary's Understanding of the Promise

In her Magnificat, Mary praises God "for remembering His mercy... as He spoke to our fathers, to Abraham and his offspring" (Luke 1:54-55), indicating her awareness that the child she bore was the fulfillment of God's covenant promises. Culturally, a virgin conceiving a child by God's Spirit was unprecedented ‚Äî yet there was Isaiah's prophecy, which early Christians saw as a foretelling of Mary's miraculous conception.

First-century Jews also longed for the "consolation of Israel", a phrase used of the Messiah. Notably, when Mary and Joseph presented baby Jesus in the Temple, an elderly devout man named Simeon proclaimed the infant to be the salvation God prepared, "a light for revelation to the Gentiles and for glory to [God's] people Israel" (Luke 2:30-32).

Mary found herself at the very center of this long-awaited hope, cradling in her arms the one whom others had yearned to see. It's poignant to consider that Mary's own expectations had to be realigned ‚Äî the common idea of a conquering Messiah would give way to the reality of a suffering Savior.

"God has brought down the mighty...and exalted those of humble estate"

- From Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:52)

In summary, Mary's background highlights how truly extraordinary her calling was. The betrothal customs and social norms of her day meant that accepting a divine pregnancy was exceedingly risky ‚Äî but Mary's faith transcended those fears. The cultural hope for a Messiah meant that Mary's yes had implications not just for her, but for her nation and the world.

Mary's Legacy

Each of these sources corroborates aspects of Mary's life and qualities, helping us form a comprehensive picture of Mary, the mother of Jesus, as a model of faith and a key figure in God's redemptive plan. Indeed, as a young woman chosen by God to bear the Messiah, Mary of Nazareth holds a unique place in Christianity. But far from being a distant, untouchable figure, Mary is profoundly human and relatable. Her story encourages us that God's grace can work through any of us, no matter how ordinary, to bring about His extraordinary purposes, if we, like Mary, are willing to trust Him and say "yes."
