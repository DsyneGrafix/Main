Understanding Scripture: Symbols, Numbers, and Meaning

Essential Tools for Biblical Interpretation and Deep Study

Biblical interpretation requires more than just reading the text. Scripture is rich with layers of meaning expressed through numbers, symbols, and linguistic patterns that span cultures and millennia. Understanding these interpretive tools transforms casual Bible reading into serious scholarly study.

This comprehensive guide introduces four essential areas every serious Bible student should master: Strong's concordance numbers for original language study, cross-referencing techniques for letting Scripture interpret Scripture, biblical numerology for understanding symbolic patterns, and scriptural symbolism for deeper thematic connections.

Whether you're a seminary student, pastor, or dedicated layperson, these tools will unlock dimensions of Scripture you never knew existed. Combined with archaeological evidence and historical context, they provide a comprehensive foundation for serious biblical interpretation.

Strong's Concordance Numbers: Your Gateway to Original Languages

What Are Strong's Numbers?

Strong's numbers are a reference system that connects every word in the King James Bible to its original Hebrew or Greek term. Created by James Strong in 1890, this system assigns a unique number to each root word in the original biblical languages.

For example, the English word "love" might translate several different Greek words with distinct meanings. Strong's numbers help you identify which specific Greek or Hebrew word was used in each biblical passage.

Why Strong's Numbers Matter for Bible Study

Most Christians read the Bible in translation, but every translation involves interpretation. Strong's numbers allow you to:

  • Discover nuanced meanings lost in translation
  • Compare word usage across different biblical passages
  • Study etymology and root meanings of ancient Hebrew and Greek terms
  • Understand cultural context of biblical expressions
  • Identify translation choices and their theological implications
  • Connect with original languages used in Gospel accounts and Hebrew Scriptures

Real Example: John 3:16 "Love"

English: "For God so loved the world..."

Strong's #25 (agapao): Divine, sacrificial, unconditional love

Insight: This isn't romantic love (eros) or friendship (phileo), but the highest form of love—God's perfect, selfless love for humanity.

How to Use Strong's Numbers: Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Find the Strong's number in your reference Bible or online tool (like Blue Letter Bible)
  2. Look up the number in Strong's Hebrew/Greek dictionary
  3. Read the definition and pronunciation guide
  4. Study the root word and related terms
  5. Compare usage in other biblical passages
  6. Apply the deeper meaning to your interpretation

Important Caveat

Strong's numbers are based on the King James Bible text and reflect 19th-century scholarship. While incredibly valuable, they should be supplemented with modern lexicons and commentaries for the most accurate understanding of ancient languages.

Biblical Numerology: Numbers with Divine Significance

Understanding Biblical Number Symbolism

Biblical numerology studies the symbolic meaning of numbers in Scripture. Unlike secular numerology or mysticism, biblical numerology focuses on how God uses specific numbers to convey theological truths and spiritual principles throughout the Bible.

These patterns appear consistently across different books, authors, and time periods, suggesting intentional divine design rather than coincidence. From the temple's precise measurements to the Passover's seven-day celebration, numbers carry theological significance that enriches our understanding of God's character and purposes.

Key Principles of Biblical Numbers

Numbers Have Theological Significance

In ancient Hebrew culture, numbers weren't just mathematical—they carried deep spiritual meaning. Understanding these meanings helps unlock layers of biblical truth.

Patterns Reveal Divine Design

When the same number appears repeatedly in similar contexts throughout Scripture, it suggests God is communicating specific concepts through numerical symbolism.

Context Matters

While numbers have general symbolic meanings, their specific interpretation must always consider the immediate biblical context and overall scriptural themes.

How to Study Biblical Numbers Responsibly

Avoid These Common Mistakes

  • Don't force numerical meanings where none exist
  • Don't use numerology to predict the future
  • Don't ignore the literal meaning in favor of symbolic interpretation
  • Don't base doctrine solely on numerical patterns

Sound Approach to Biblical Number Study

  1. Study the literal context first - What is the passage actually saying?
  2. Look for repeated patterns - Does this number appear significantly elsewhere?
  3. Consider cultural background - What did numbers mean in ancient Hebrew/Greek culture?
  4. Compare with clear teaching - Does the symbolic interpretation align with clear biblical doctrine?
  5. Apply with wisdom - How does this enhance understanding of God's character and purposes?

Cross-Referencing: Let Scripture Interpret Scripture

The Foundation of Sound Biblical Interpretation

Cross-referencing is the most fundamental technique in biblical interpretation—using Scripture to explain Scripture. Rather than imposing our own interpretations, cross-referencing allows the Bible to be its own interpreter by comparing related passages, themes, and concepts throughout the biblical canon.

This method recognizes that the Holy Spirit is the ultimate author of all Scripture, ensuring internal consistency and mutual illumination between different biblical texts, whether written centuries apart or by different human authors.

Why Cross-Referencing Is Essential

Cross-referencing prevents misinterpretation by providing biblical context for understanding difficult or unclear passages. It reveals how biblical themes develop progressively from Genesis to Revelation and ensures that our interpretations align with the broader teaching of Scripture.

Key Benefits of Systematic Cross-Referencing:

  • Prevents isolated interpretations that ignore broader biblical context
  • Reveals progressive revelation - how God unfolds truth over time
  • Builds doctrine on multiple witnesses rather than single verses
  • Clarifies difficult passages using clearer biblical texts
  • Exposes false teachings that contradict Scripture's overall message
  • Enriches understanding through cumulative biblical insight

Types of Cross-References

Verbal Cross-References

These connect passages using the same Hebrew or Greek words. Archaeological discoveries have confirmed the precision of biblical language, making verbal cross-references particularly valuable for understanding original meanings.

Example: "Righteousness" (Greek: dikaiosyne)

Connecting Romans 3:21, Romans 10:3, 2 Corinthians 5:21, and Philippians 3:9 reveals how Paul develops the concept of imputed righteousness throughout his letters.

Thematic Cross-References

These link passages sharing common themes or concepts, even when different vocabulary is used. Understanding Jewish roots of Christianity helps identify thematic connections between Old and New Testament passages.

Doctrinal Cross-References

These gather all passages teaching about specific doctrines like salvation, sanctification, or the nature of Christ. This comprehensive approach prevents building doctrine on insufficient biblical foundation.

Historical Cross-References

These connect events, people, and places across different biblical books. The temple studies and tabernacle analysis demonstrate how cross-referencing historical elements reveals typological meanings.

Cross-Referencing Methods and Tools

Manual Cross-Reference Method

  1. Identify key terms in your passage for Strong's number lookup
  2. Use a concordance to find all occurrences of those terms
  3. Read each related passage in its own context
  4. Note similarities and differences in usage and meaning
  5. Look for progressive development of the concept
  6. Synthesize insights for comprehensive understanding

Case Study: Understanding "Faith" Through Cross-Reference

Starting Point: Hebrews 11:1 - "Faith is the substance of things hoped for..."

Cross-References: Romans 4:16-25 (Abraham's faith), James 2:14-26 (faith and works), Ephesians 2:8-9 (faith and grace)

Cross-Referencing Cautions

  • Don't force connections between unrelated passages
  • Maintain attention to individual passage contexts
  • Distinguish between illustration and interpretation
  • Be aware of progressive revelation - later texts clarify earlier ones
  • Use cross-references to support, not replace, careful exegesis

Biblical Symbols and Metaphors

The Language of Symbols in Scripture

The Bible uses rich symbolic language to communicate spiritual truths that transcend simple literal description. From the Garden of Eden's trees to Revelation's cosmic imagery, symbols help us understand invisible spiritual realities through familiar physical objects and concepts.

Mastering biblical symbolism transforms how you read both Old and New Testament passages, revealing connections and themes that span the entire biblical narrative. Cross-referencing symbolic usage throughout Scripture prevents misinterpretation and reveals intended meanings.

Major Categories of Biblical Symbols

Nature Symbols

  • Water: Life, cleansing, the Holy Spirit, God's Word (see baptism symbolism in Passover)
  • Fire: God's presence, judgment, purification, the Holy Spirit
  • Light: Truth, revelation, Christ, righteousness
  • Darkness: Sin, ignorance, evil, separation from God
  • Bread: Sustenance, God's provision, Christ as life-giver (connect to Passover unleavened bread)

Architectural Symbols

  • Temple: God's presence, the believer's body, the church (explore temple symbolism in depth)
  • Door/Gate: Christ as the way to salvation
  • Foundation: Christ, apostolic teaching, stability
  • Corner Stone: Christ as the essential building block
  • Veil: Separation between holy and common study Holy of Holies significance

Tabernacle and Temple Symbolism

The Tabernacle's intricate design provides some of Scripture's richest symbolic teaching. Every element from the bronze altar to the Holy of Holies points to Christ's redemptive work. Understanding these connections enhances interpretation of both Old Testament worship passages and New Testament references to Christ as our High Priest.

Human Relationships

  • Marriage: Christ and the church, God and Israel
  • Father: God's loving authority and provision (see Abraham as father of faith)
  • Shepherd: Leadership, care, guidance (Christ, pastors)
  • King: Authority, rule, sovereignty

Symbol in Action: Jesus as "The Vine" (John 15)

Symbol: Vine and branches

Cultural Context: Agriculture was central to ancient life; everyone understood vine cultivation

Spiritual Truth: Believers depend completely on Christ for spiritual life and fruit-bearing

Practical Application: Apart from Christ, we can accomplish nothing of eternal value

Putting It All Together: Integrated Bible Study

The most powerful biblical interpretation happens when you combine these tools systematically. Here's how to integrate Strong's numbers, cross-referencing, biblical numerology, and symbolic interpretation for deeper study that builds on the foundation provided in our comprehensive theological education guide:

Complete Study Method

  1. Read the passage carefully in its immediate context
  2. Identify key words for Strong's number investigation
  3. Cross-reference related passages using verbal, thematic, and doctrinal connections
  4. Look for numerical patterns and their potential significance
  5. Note symbolic language and metaphorical expressions (connect to tabernacle symbolism when relevant)
  6. Compare with historical context using archaeological evidence
  7. Synthesize insights into a cohesive understanding
  8. Apply the truth to your life and ministry context

Case Study: Psalm 23

Strong's Study: "Shepherd" (H7462) means to feed, tend, graze—implying intimate care

Numerical Significance: Psalm 23 has exactly 55 Hebrew words (5x11), numbers of grace and judgment

Symbols: Shepherd-sheep relationship, green pastures (provision), still waters (peace), rod and staff (discipline and guidance)

Integration: God provides intimate, complete care for His people through every circumstance of life
