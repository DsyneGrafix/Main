Introduction to Systematic Theology

Systematic theology is the study of what the whole Bible teaches on key topics (often called doctrines) and how those truths fit together. Instead of reading Scripture in chronological order, systematic theology organizes beliefs by theme â€“ for example, gathering everything the Bible says about God, or about salvation, into a coherent summary.

This approach helps answer life's big questions â€“ Who is God? Why are we here? What is God's plan for the world? â€“ in a unified way. In short, theology means "studying God" and all that He has revealed about Himself and His purposes. Every Christian is a "theologian" at some level, because what we believe about God affects how we live.

Key Terms in Plain English:

Doctrine - A teaching or belief
Systematic Theology - Organizing biblical teachings by topics
Eschatology - The study of "last things" or end times

Focus on Eschatology â€“ Digging Deeper into the End Times

Eschatology can be one of the most fascinating doctrines for new students â€“ and one of the most confusing! In this special focus section, we provide guidance for going deeper on the end times in a healthy, beginner-friendly way.

Keep the "Main Hope" in View

All Christians share a common hope regarding the end times: Jesus will return, evil will be defeated, God's justice will prevail, and God's people will enjoy His presence forever. Any deeper study of eschatology should keep these core truths front and center.

Understanding Different Views Charitably

Sincere Christians interpret some prophetic details differently. If you want to dig into these views, approach the topic with humility and an open mind. A recommended beginner resource is "A Basic Guide to Eschatology" by Millard J. Erickson which outlines the major positions side by side.

Life After "The End"

A wonderful aspect of eschatology to explore is what happens after Christ returns â€“ namely, the doctrine of the new creation. "Surprised by Hope" by N.T. Wright challenges popular folk ideas about heaven and emphasizes that the ultimate Christian hope is bodily resurrection and the renewal of the whole world.

Guided and Self-Paced Learning Options

Guided Instruction (Group Classes & Courses)

If you prefer a structured class or learn well with others, consider joining a church-based theology class or study group. Many churches offer courses like "Foundations of the Faith" or a systematic theology overview for newcomers.

Online Course Options:

The Gospel Coalition: "Basics of Systematic Theology" (free course with video lectures)
Ligonier Ministries: "Foundations: An Overview of Systematic Theology" by R.C. Sproul

Self-Paced Study (Independent Learning)

If a formal class isn't feasible, you can absolutely study systematic theology on your own schedule. The key is to have a plan and good resources. You can follow the 10-week outline above, but adjust the pace as needed.

Recommended Books for Ongoing Study

Everyone's a Theologian by R.C. Sproul

A concise systematic theology for laypeople. Sproul was known for clarity and "making theology practical." This book covers all major doctrines in ~300 pages and is written in a warm, narrative style.

Christian Beliefs: Twenty Basics Every Christian Should Know by Wayne Grudem

Only ~160 pages, this is a simplified version of Grudem's larger works. As Dr. Al Mohler notes, Grudem explains "profound truths in simple language." Each short chapter includes questions for review.

Concise Theology by J.I. Packer

94 brief chapters (~2 pages each) on different doctrines. Packer's style is elegant yet plainspoken. This book serves almost like a devotional; you can read a topic a day.
