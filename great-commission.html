The Great Commission

Understanding Christ's Final Command to His Disciples

The Great Commission stands as one of the most significant and enduring commands in human history. Spoken by Jesus Christ to His disciples following His resurrection, these words have shaped the course of Christianity for over two millennia and continue to drive believers worldwide in their mission to spread the Gospel message.

This comprehensive examination explores the historical context, theological significance, practical implications, and modern applications of Christ's final earthly directive to His followers.

Historical Context and Setting

The Great Commission is recorded in Matthew 28:16-20, though similar accounts appear in Mark 16:15-16, Luke 24:44-49, and Acts 1:8. Matthew's account provides the most detailed version and serves as the foundation for understanding this pivotal moment in Christian history.

The setting is a mountain in Galilee, where Jesus had arranged to meet His eleven remaining disciples following His resurrection from the dead. This encounter occurs approximately forty days after Jesus' crucifixion and resurrection, during a period when He appeared to His followers multiple times to confirm His victory over death and to prepare them for their future ministry.

Historical Significance

The location is significantâ€”Galilee was where Jesus had called most of His disciples and conducted much of His earthly ministry. By returning to this familiar region, Jesus was connecting His final instructions with the beginning of their journey together.

The Disciples' State of Mind

Matthew's account reveals that when the disciples saw Jesus, they worshiped Him, but some doubted. This honest portrayal of the disciples' mixed emotions provides crucial context for understanding the Great Commission.

These men had witnessed Jesus' crucifixion, experienced the trauma of His death, and then encountered Him alive again. Their doubt was not necessarily skepticism about Jesus' identity, but rather uncertainty about what His resurrection meant for their future and the continuation of His mission.

Analyzing the Command: Four Essential Elements

The Great Commission contains four distinct but interconnected elements that form a comprehensive strategy for spreading the Christian faith. Understanding each component is essential for grasping the full scope of Jesus' instructions.

1. The Foundation: "All Authority Has Been Given to Me"

Jesus begins His commission not with a command but with a declaration of His authority. The Greek word used here is exousia, which refers to both the right to exercise power and the actual power itself.

Key Insight: By claiming "all authority in heaven and on earth," Jesus establishes the basis upon which His followers can carry out their mission with confidence. This divine authorization transforms the Great Commission from human ambition to divine mandate.

The comprehensiveness of this authorityâ€”covering both heaven and earthâ€”indicates that there are no geographical, political, or spiritual limitations to the mission Jesus is entrusting to His disciples.

2. The Action: "Go and Make Disciples"

The central command of the Great Commission is often translated as "go," but the Greek construction places the emphasis on "make disciples." The word "go" (poreuomai) is actually a participle that could be translated as "as you go" or "while going."

This suggests that disciple-making should be a natural part of believers' lives as they move through their daily activities and travels. The primary verb is "make disciples" (matheteuo), which means much more than simply converting people to Christianity.

Understanding Discipleship

A disciple is a learner, follower, and imitator of a teacher. Making disciples involves helping people not just to believe in Jesus but to follow Him as Lord, learn His teachings, and live according to His example.

3. The Scope: "All Nations"

The phrase "all nations" (panta ta ethne in Greek) literally means "all the ethnic groups" or "all peoples." This was a revolutionary concept in the first-century Jewish context, where God's covenant was primarily understood as being with the nation of Israel.

Jesus was explicitly extending the scope of salvation and discipleship to include every ethnic group and culture on earth. This universal scope reflects God's original intention, as revealed in His promise to Abraham that "all peoples on earth will be blessed through you" (Genesis 12:3).

4. The Process: Baptizing and Teaching

Jesus outlines a two-part process for making disciples: baptizing and teaching. These elements represent the initial commitment and ongoing development aspects of discipleship.

Baptizing represents the public declaration of faith and the symbolic entry into the Christian community. The Trinitarian formulaâ€”"in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit"â€”emphasizes the theological foundation of Christian faith.

Teaching represents the ongoing process of spiritual development. Jesus specifies that new disciples should be taught "to obey everything I have commanded you." This is not merely intellectual instruction but practical training in Christian living.

Contemporary Applications for Modern Believers

The Great Commission is not merely a historical document or a directive for professional missionaries. It remains a living command that applies to every follower of Jesus Christ in the contemporary world.

Modern Reality: Every Christian has opportunities to fulfill the Great Commission through personal evangelism, supporting mission work, making disciples in local contexts, and developing cross-cultural sensitivity and understanding.

Personal Evangelism and Witness

Every Christian has opportunities to fulfill the Great Commission through personal evangelism and witness. This doesn't necessarily mean preaching on street corners or going door-to-door, though those approaches may be appropriate in some contexts.

More often, it involves building authentic relationships with non-Christians and sharing the Gospel naturally as opportunities arise. Effective personal evangelism requires both intentionality and sensitivity.

Making Disciples in Local Contexts

The Great Commission emphasizes not just evangelism but discipleship. This means that Christians have a responsibility to help new believers grow in their faith and become mature disciples who can, in turn, make other disciples.

Local church discipleship might involve mentoring new believers, leading small group Bible studies, or participating in structured discipleship programs. The goal is to help people move beyond initial conversion to become committed followers of Jesus.

The Continuing Relevance of Christ's Commission

The Great Commission remains as relevant and urgent today as it was when Jesus first spoke these words to His disciples nearly two thousand years ago. The fundamental human need for salvation has not changed, and Christ's command to make disciples continues to define the mission of the Church.

Contemporary Christians face unique challenges and opportunities in fulfilling this mission. Globalization, technology, and cultural changes have created new contexts for evangelism and discipleship, but they have not altered the basic content or urgency of the Great Commission.

Eternal Promise: The promise that accompanies the Great Commissionâ€”"And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age"â€”provides both comfort and confidence for this ongoing mission. Christ's presence with His disciples means that they are not undertaking this mission in their own strength but in the power and authority of the risen Lord.

The Great Commission ultimately represents more than a command to be obeyed; it represents an invitation to participate in God's ongoing work of redemption in the world. For those who accept this invitation, it provides purpose, direction, and meaning that transcends the temporary concerns of earthly life and connects them to the eternal purposes of God.

As the Church continues to grow and spread throughout the world, the Great Commission remains the foundational mission that unites all believers across cultural, denominational, and geographical boundaries. It reminds every Christian that they have a role to play in God's redemptive plan and that their faithfulness in sharing the Gospel and making disciples has eternal significance.
