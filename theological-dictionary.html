A

Agnosticism Philosophy

Definition: The view that one does not know whether there is a God or not. Agnostics maintain that the existence or non-existence of God cannot be proven or known.

Etymology: From Greek "a-" (without) + "gnosis" (knowledge)

Example: "Thomas Huxley coined the term 'agnostic' in 1869, distinguishing it from both theism and atheism."

Related Terms: Theism, Gnosticism

Aseity Divine Attributes

Definition: The property of not depending on any cause other than itself for its existence. God's self-existence and independence from all other beings.

Etymology: From Latin "a se" meaning "from itself"

Key Verses: Exodus 3:14 "I AM WHO I AM"; John 5:26; Acts 17:25

Example: "God's aseity means He is the uncaused cause, existing eternally without beginning or dependence."

D

Deity General Theology

Definition: A supernatural being, often referred to as "god" (with lowercase g). In Christianity, refers to the divine nature or essence of God.

Key Verses: Colossians 2:9; Romans 1:20

Demiurge Gnosticism

Definition: In Gnostic cosmogony, often depicted as an evil lesser deity or creator god of the Old Testament, distinct from the true, transcendent God.

Etymology: From Greek "demiourgos" meaning "craftsman" or "artisan"

Example: "Gnostics viewed the demiurge as responsible for creating the flawed material world, while the true God remained purely spiritual."

Divine Foreknowledge Divine Attributes

Definition: God's knowledge of future events before they occur. The doctrine that God has complete knowledge of all that will happen.

Key Verses: Isaiah 46:10; Psalm 139:4; Acts 2:23

Related Terms: Omniscience, Divine Providence

Divine Providence Divine Action

Definition: God's continuous involvement with creation, including His preservation, cooperation with creatures, and governance of all things toward their appointed end.

Key Verses: Matthew 10:29-31; Romans 8:28; Ephesians 1:11

Example: "Divine providence assures believers that nothing happens outside of God's sovereign will and care."

ðŸ“š Learn More About Providence

Providence includes three aspects: preservation (maintaining creation), concurrence (cooperating with created things), and government (directing all things to their purpose).

E

Elohim Hebrew Terms

Definition: A Hebrew word, plural in form but often used with singular verbs, used in the Old Testament to refer to the true God of Israel. One of the primary names for God in Scripture.

Etymology: Hebrew plural of "Eloah" (God), possibly from root meaning "mighty" or "strong"

Key Verses: Genesis 1:1; Deuteronomy 6:4; Psalm 86:10

Example: "In Genesis 1:1, 'Elohim' is the first name used for God: 'In the beginning, Elohim created the heavens and the earth.'"

End-time (Eschatology) Prophecy

Definition: The period discussed in prophecy referring to conditions before and during the establishment of God's Kingdom on Earth. Also called "Latter Days," "Last Days," or "The Day of the Lord."

Key Verses: Daniel 12:4; Matthew 24; 2 Timothy 3:1; Revelation

Related Terms: Eschatology

F

Free Will Philosophy/Theology

Definition: The capacity of an agent to make choices in a way such that they could have done otherwise. The ability to choose between different possible courses of action unimpeded.

Key Verses: Deuteronomy 30:19; Joshua 24:15; Galatians 5:13

Example: "The debate between free will and divine sovereignty has been central to Christian theology for centuries."

ðŸ“š Theological Perspectives

Different Christian traditions hold varying views: Arminians emphasize libertarian free will, Calvinists focus on compatibilist freedom, while others seek middle ground.

G

Gnosticism Historical Movement

Definition: A religious movement emphasizing secret knowledge (gnosis) as the path to salvation, often viewing the material world as corrupt and the creator god as a lesser deity.

Etymology: From Greek "gnosis" meaning "knowledge"

Example: "Early church fathers like Irenaeus wrote extensively against Gnostic teachings that threatened orthodox Christianity."

Related Terms: Demiurge, Monad

God's Plan Divine Sovereignty

Definition: God's eternal purpose and design for creation and humanity, encompassing both His sovereign will and His redemptive purposes through Christ.

Key Verses: Ephesians 1:11; Jeremiah 29:11; Romans 8:28

M

Monad Philosophy/Gnosticism

Definition: In some philosophies and Gnosticism, a term referring to divinity, the first being, an indivisible origin, or a higher, benevolent god beyond comprehension.

Etymology: From Greek "monas" meaning "unit" or "one"

Monotheism Core Doctrine

Definition: The belief that there is only one deity, referred to as "God" (with uppercase G). The foundational belief of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.

Key Verses: Deuteronomy 6:4; Isaiah 45:5; 1 Corinthians 8:6

Related Terms: Trinity, Theism

O

Omnipotence Divine Attributes

Definition: The quality of having unlimited power. God's ability to do all things consistent with His nature and will.

Key Verses: Genesis 18:14; Matthew 19:26; Revelation 19:6

Example: "God's omnipotence doesn't mean He can do logically contradictory things (like making a square circle) but that He can do all things possible."

Omniscience Divine Attributes

Definition: The quality of having infinite knowledge. God's perfect knowledge of all things past, present, and future, actual and possible.

Key Verses: Psalm 139:1-6; 1 John 3:20; Hebrews 4:13

Related Terms: Divine Foreknowledge

Occasionalism Philosophy

Definition: The philosophical idea that the universe requires God's continuous intervention to exist from one instant to the next. Every event is directly caused by God.

Example: "Islamic philosopher Al-Ghazali and Christian philosopher Nicolas Malebranche were prominent advocates of occasionalism."

P

Panentheism Philosophy/Theology

Definition: The belief that God is in everything and everything is in God, but God is also more than the universe. God both pervades and transcends creation.

Etymology: From Greek "pan" (all) + "en" (in) + "theos" (God)

Example: "Unlike pantheism, panentheism maintains God's transcendence while affirming His intimate presence in creation."

Pantheism Philosophy/Theology

Definition: The belief that God is identical with the universe. Everything is God and God is everything.

Etymology: From Greek "pan" (all) + "theos" (God)

Related Terms: Panentheism, Theism

Permissive Will of God Divine Sovereignty

Definition: The idea that God allows certain things to happen, including sin, even if they are not His ideal will, often for the purpose of preserving free will or teaching through consequences.

Key Verses: Matthew 19:8; Acts 14:16; Romans 1:24-28

Example: "God's permissive will allowed Joseph's brothers to sell him into slavery, but God used it for good (Genesis 50:20)."

Problem of Evil Philosophy/Theodicy

Definition: The philosophical and theological question of how an all-powerful, all-knowing, and perfectly good God can coexist with the existence of evil and suffering in the world.

Example: "The problem of evil has been addressed through various theodicies, including free will defense, soul-making theodicy, and the greater good argument."

Related Terms: Theodicy, Soul-making

ðŸ“š Major Responses to the Problem

1. Free Will Defense: Evil results from human free choice
2. Soul-Making: Suffering develops character
3. Greater Good: Some evils serve greater purposes
4. Skeptical Theism: We can't fully understand God's reasons

S

Sanctification Salvation

Definition: The process of being made holy or set apart for God's purposes. The ongoing transformation of believers into the likeness of Christ.

Etymology: From Latin "sanctus" (holy) + "facere" (to make)

Key Verses: 1 Thessalonians 4:3; 2 Corinthians 3:18; Philippians 2:12-13

Example: "Sanctification is both positional (set apart at conversion) and progressive (growing in holiness throughout life)."

Related Terms: Justification, Salvation

Satan (The Adversary) Spiritual Beings

Definition: Identified in the Bible as the "prince of the power of the air" and the ruler of this world, a deceiver, murderer, and destroyer. The chief opponent of God and His people.

Etymology: From Hebrew "satan" meaning "adversary" or "accuser"

Key Verses: Job 1-2; Matthew 4:1-11; Revelation 12:9; 1 Peter 5:8

Sin Core Doctrine

Definition: Lawlessness or the transgression of God's law or commandments. Any thought, word, or deed that falls short of God's perfect standard.

Etymology: Hebrew "chata" (to miss the mark); Greek "hamartia" (missing the target)

Key Verses: 1 John 3:4; Romans 3:23; James 4:17

Example: "Sin includes both commission (doing wrong) and omission (failing to do right)."

Soul-making Theodicy

Definition: A theological concept suggesting that suffering and challenges contribute to the development of godly character and spiritual maturity in human beings.

Key Verses: Romans 5:3-4; James 1:2-4; 2 Corinthians 4:17

Related Terms: Problem of Evil, Theodicy

Special Providence Divine Action

Definition: Extraordinary intervention by God in human affairs, such as miracles, distinct from God's ordinary providence in sustaining creation.

Key Verses: Exodus 14:21-22; 2 Kings 20:1-11; Acts 12:5-11

Related Terms: Divine Providence

T

Tawhid Islamic Theology

Definition: An Islamic concept meaning 'oneness' or 'uniqueness' of God. The indivisible oneness concept of monotheism in Islam.

Etymology: From Arabic root "wahada" meaning "to unite" or "to make one"

Example: "While Christians affirm monotheism through Trinity, Muslims emphasize absolute divine unity through Tawhid."

Teleological Purpose Philosophy

Definition: The ultimate end or goal towards which something is directed or exists. The study of purpose, design, and goals in nature and existence.

Etymology: From Greek "telos" (end, goal) + "logos" (study)

Key Verses: Romans 8:28; Ephesians 1:11; Colossians 1:16

Theism Core Belief

Definition: The belief in the existence of a god or gods, especially belief in one God as creator of the universe, intervening in it and sustaining a personal relation to His creatures.

Related Terms: Monotheism, Pantheism, Agnosticism

Theodicy Philosophy/Theology

Definition: An attempt to justify the ways of God to humanity, specifically addressing how a good and omnipotent God can permit evil and suffering.

Etymology: From Greek "theos" (God) + "dikÄ“" (justice)

Key Verses: Job 1-42; Romans 8:28; 2 Corinthians 12:7-10

Example: "The book of Job is Scripture's primary theodicy, exploring why righteous people suffer."

Related Terms: Problem of Evil, Soul-making

Theos Greek Terms

Definition: The Greek word used in the New Testament to translate the Hebrew term Elohim, meaning God.

Key Verses: John 1:1; Romans 1:7; 1 Corinthians 8:6

Related Terms: Elohim

Trinity Core Doctrine

Definition: The doctrine in mainstream Christianity that God is three distinct persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) in a single divine essence or being.

Etymology: From Latin "trinitas" meaning "triad" or "threefold"

Key Verses: Matthew 28:19; 2 Corinthians 13:14; 1 Peter 1:2

Example: "The Trinity doctrine maintains that God is one in essence but three in persons, each fully God yet distinct."

Related Terms: Monotheism, Theos

ðŸ“š Understanding the Trinity

The Trinity is not: 3 gods (tritheism), 1 God in 3 modes (modalism), or 3 parts of God.
The Trinity is: One God eternally existing as three distinct persons who share the same divine essence.
